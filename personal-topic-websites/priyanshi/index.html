Types of Universities in the U.S.

If you’re not familiar with the US university system, things can get a little confusing, particularly when it comes to working out how much your studies are likely to cost. Here’s a quick overview of the main different types of university you can expect to encounter in the US.

  • Public Universities

    • In the United States, public colleges, often known as state universities, receive some public support from the state government. State institutions often charge cheaper tuition rates than private universities since they are financed by public funds - though the actual amount students pay can vary greatly. There are two fee bands listed: one for "resident" or "in-state" students and another for "non-resident" or "out-of-state" candidates. International students either pay the same fees as out-of-state students, or a separate international rate.

  • Community collges

    • Also referred to as junior colleges, community colleges are also supported by state funding. They typically offer the chance to complete an associate’s degree, an undergraduate-level qualification which takes two years to complete. Community colleges tend to be on the smaller side, and their tuition fees are typically much lower than those at other types of university in the US. Like public universities, they’re sometimes grouped into state “systems”.

  • Private non-profit US universities

    • Also referred to as junior colleges, community colleges are also supported by state funding. They typically offer the chance to complete an associate’s degree, an undergraduate-level qualification which takes two years to complete. Community colleges tend to be on the smaller side, and their tuition fees are typically much lower than those at other types of university in the US. Like public universities, they’re sometimes grouped into state “systems”.

  • Liberal arts colleges

    • These collges offer a broad education in arts and sciences subjects that are designed to give undergraduates academic range as well as depth.

    How to get student visa !

    You must apply for the F-1 visa in order to study at a US college. You will be registered for the Student and Exchange Visitor Information System (SEVIS) and must pay the $350 SEVIS I-901 fee. Once you have been accepted at a US college accredited by the Student and Exchange Visitor Program (SEVP). The Form I-20 will be sent to you after that, and you will need to register with SEVIS. You can apply for your visa at a US embassy or consulate once you've completed these steps. Depending on where you apply, you may be charged an additional minor administration fee in addition to the $160 visa application fee. You'll also need to fill out another DS-160 form.

    Language Test

    Most residents of the United States require international applicants to take a test to demonstrate their English language proficiency, such as the TOEFL or IELTS academic.

    Cost to study in the U.S.

    The cost of tuition in the United States ranges from $5,000 to $50,000 (£4,074 to £40,746) a year. Students graduate with an average debt of $132,860 (£101,505). Beside the tuition fees, students also need to get the external cost like housing, food and of course their personal needs.
